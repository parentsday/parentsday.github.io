Lecture on BCI
HG 00.65

Bridging artificial intelligence and cognitive neuroscience: from reading images to reading brains.

In the last couple of years quite some progress has been made within artificial intelligence so that nowadays it is possible to learn complex patterns from large amounts of data. For instance, making use of what is called “deep learning”, we are able to recognize objects and faces from 2D images at levels that even surpass human performance. These type of models are largely inspired by how the brain works, and a fruitful way to improve these models even further is to transfer knowledge about the brain to the artificial counterpart. In a similar vein, we can use these models to study the brain and test specific hypotheses, providing us with new insights about the workings of the brain. Thus, with this circular process we open up new insights not only in artificial intelligence but also cognitive neuroscience. Additionally, many applications emerge from natural language processing to self-driving cars, to exciting areas like automated medical diagnosis to brain reading and brain-computer interfaces. In this talk I will show how artificial intelligence and cognitive neuroscience are so intertwined and I will provide a short overview of the state-of-the-art in several of these exciting applications.

Learn about our machine counterparts

Luc Selen

HG 0.307

Do you actually know what a robot is? Is your refrigerator a robot? And what about your washing machine? We use robots in our daily life these days even though you might not realise it. But this is not the main application of robotics. They’re increasingly more used in healthcare, factories and entertainment. They are presented as faultless creatures in the media but this is definitely not true. The basic human tasks are still very difficult for robots. Such things as opening a door, walking a staircase, picking up a cup and even just walking.
